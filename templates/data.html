Data Analysis Completion Rate Map

This figure shows the average college completion rate calculated for all institutions in a given state of mainland United States. The figure shows information for a 10 years period spanning from 2007 to 2016. Out of all the states, only 16 showed a positive trend while the remaining 35 showed a negative one. This seems to suggest that there might be a negative factor(s) decreasing students success in completing a college degree in the last decade.

Data Analysis Univariate Plots

Key observations about the univariate plots:

(a) Almost 50% of schools are located in either the New England, Mid East or South East region. This regions include states such as New York, Pennsylvania, Florida, Massachusetts, North Carolina, and Georgia which are in the top 10 states with most schools in the nation.

(b) Above 80% of schools have a higher than 50% admission rate. Institutions such as Caltech, Yale, Harvard, MIT, Princeton, Columbia, and Stanford fall into the lower than 10% admission rate.

(c) Similar to admission rate, retention rate above 50% is typical in USA institutions. However, in 69 schools only 1 out of every 3 students keeps persuing an undergraduate degree after the first year of enrollement.

(d) Completion rates follow a surprisingly normal distribution. Institutions like Caltech, Yale, Harvard, and MIT have a higher than 90% completion rate. This seems to indicate that once someone enters into a top school is likely to finish it.

Data Analysis Correlation Matrix

This matrix shows the correlations between 9 features of the College Scorecards dataset. Overall, the biggest correlations are among In-State tuition and cost of attendance with 0.97 and between In-State and Out-of-State tuition with 0.9. For the particular case of completion rate, the largest correlations include: retention rate, In- and Out- of state tuition, and cost of attendance.

Data Analysis Scatter Plots

This plots shows the dependencies of completion rate against 4 different features in the dataset. Top and bottoms figures show correlations for public and private schools respectively. As expected, retention rate shows an explicit linear relationship with completion rate, especially when looking at public schools.
